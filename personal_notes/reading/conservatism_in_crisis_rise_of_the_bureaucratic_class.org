#+TITLE: Conservatism In Crisis: Rise of the Bureaucratic Class

* Introduction, by Kemi Badenoch
- The Conservative Party in the UK has made many mistakes
- Many of these mistakes will be repeated and doubled down upon by the new Labor government
- Since 1992, the Conservative Party has only won elections when Brexit was an issue
- The Conservative Party has to refocus around ideology, rather than policy
- Progressives have adopted an ideology consisting of the following two pillars
  - Constant government intervention is necessary to protect marginalized groups
  - Rule-following bureaucrats can make better decisions than individuals or democratic forms of governance
- This ideology is being driven by a new bureaucratic class
  - /They're not new, not by a long shot/
  - /You're only noticing them now/
- This is responsible for the twin phenomena of increasing identity politics and increasing government intervention in all aspects of daily life
- Many of these changes are actually driven by private-sector organizations who administer government rules --- the old "public/private distinction" no longer applies
  - Difference between a private lawyer focusing on contract between two private sector entities and a private lawyer focusing on compliance issues
  - Difference between a HR department focused on hiring great talent and an HR department focused on DEI issues
- Recently, there was a paper, /Foundations/, which listed the tens of thousands of pages of rules and procedure that must be followed in order to build infrastructure
- The Conservative Party has been nowhere to be seen when it comes to challenging and rolling back these rules
- The Conservative Party needs to focus on advertising a new culture for Britain, not just new policies
  - Challenge the idea that bureaucracy knows best
  - Challenge the idea that nation-states with borders are fundamentally unjust
- The Conservative Party needs to rise to the challenge posed by this progressive ideology rather than simply retreating to an imagined vision of the past
- Need to challenge Labor's vision of a managerial future

* Executive Summary
- In July 2024, the Conservative Party received the lowest number of MPs and the lowest share of the popular vote in its 200 year history
- The only general election victories that the Conservative Party has won since 1992 have been in 2015 and 2019, when Brexit was a salient issue

** The rise of the new progressive left among the West
- Brexit is a reflection and symptom, not a cause, of a political realignment
- Conservatives have failed to acknowledge the rise of a new bureaucratic middle class
- The ideology of this class is distinct from either conservatives or socialists
  - Due to ever-present inherently unfair power structures, the government needs to constantly redistribute economic benefits to marginalized communities
  - Marginalized communities consist of the poor, but also certain other classes deemed "vulnerable"
    - Women
    - LGBT
    - Ethnic and religious minorities
    - Disabled and neuro-diverse
    - Migrants
  - Progressive bureaucrats can make better decisions on a wide range of issues than either individuals or democratic institutions
  - Therefore decision-making authority should be taken away from individuals and democratically elected bodies and concentrated in the hands of rule-following bureaucrats
- /This is one of the best and most succinct definitions of "woke" I've seen so far/
- This ideology builds on Marxist roots, but transcends Marxism
- Calls for constant policing of society to redress these perceived imbalances

** The rise of a new bureaucratic class
- The bureaucratic class receives both income and justification from the ever growing regulatory state
- The microeconomic link between the government and the private sector bureaucratic class can be assessed by looking at jobs or companies and asking whether their primary role is the provision of goods and services in the market or the administration rules set out by the government
- Difference between market and bureaucratic classes
  - Market class: give people what they demand and will pay for
  - Bureaucratic class: give people what the government requires them to have, and what they think that people should want
- Increasing numbers of middle-class jobs are dependent entirely on the existence of government rules
- This close relationship with government and its regulations creates a different reality for much of the urban middle class
- This change was noticed and cited as early as the 1940s, by James Burnam in /The Managerial Revolution/
- This class took over the left after the fall of the Soviet Union discredited socialism in the West

** A fundamental political realignment
- Goodwin and Goodhart lay out some of the crucial characteristics of this class
  - Focus on credentials
  - Links with rising levels of "professionalism"
  - Focus on administrative control of workspaces
- However, Goodwin and Goodhart miss the relationship this class has with government
- The dependence this class has on government explains why higher-educated voters have moved from being predominantly right-wing to being predominantly left-wing
- This split in the middle class between market-orientation and government-orientation is erasing the link between income and conservatism
- Rising income is no longer a sign of voters becoming more conservative
- This is true across the West, most notably in the United States
- What matters now is /how/ you earn your money, now /how much/ you earn
- There is no longer a "generational" divide between left and right

** The bureaucratic class benefit from the left-wing view that "more government is best"
- Bureaucrats, both in the public sector and private sector, benefit from more government and more regulation, not less
- Bureaucrats' jobs are about protecting people and enforcing regulations, so they benefit from there being more regulation

*** Human resources and the increasing regulation of the labor market
- In 2001 there were roughly 119,000 people in the UK working as human resources managers
- By 2023, this had risen to 221,000, an increase of 86%
- The government is indirectly responsible for this increase, by mandating diversity and equity requirements
  - Financial Conduct Authority pushes for firms to make 40% of their board directors women
  - Government regulations make high standards illegal if they are "experienced as intimidation"
- Bureaucracy rewards specific ideas related to equality of outcomes rather than productivity

*** The university explosion
- The number of university graduates increased from 77,000 a year in 1990 to 750,000 a year by 2020
- The number of people receiving postgraduate degrees increased from 31,000 in 1990 to 493,000 in 2020
- The increase in the number of university students has been accompanied by an even greater rise in university staff
- Only half of the 500,000 or so people employed in universities are academics --- the rest are bureaucrats and administrators
- The state has backed the explosion of university degrees by underwriting university educations and making degrees mandatory for certain professions

*** The socialization of mental health
- Enforcing special treatment for people with mental health disorders has failed to improve outcomes
- The meaning of "safety" has undergone concept creep and now encompasses emotional safety
- Harm has become a subjective criterion
- Neurodiversity has become a protected class, akin to race and gender
  - Can more easily make claims for unfair dismissal
  - Can require that employers make "reasonable adjustments" to working conditions, adjustments which can be revealed after you've been hired
- By 2021 mental health was the #1 source of new welfare claims, as well as being the source of 41% of all new disability claims

*** The vast growth of regulatory and compliance sectors
- The ratio of regulators to workers was roughly 1:11,000 in 1980
- Today it stands at 1:75
- In 10 years, Citigroup has had to increase the proportion of its workforce dedicated to compliance and risk management from 4% to 15%
- For banks, compliance activities cost 6-10% of revenues
- The legal profession has also grown --- 700% increase in the number of lawyers, as compared with a 200% increase in GDP
- New legal specialties having entirely to do with government bureaucracy, such as environmental law, human rights law and immigration and refugee law are now viable career paths for lawyers

** A paradigm shift in the function of society and the economy
- These changes are creating a paradigm shift in the relationship between society and the economy
- Bureaucrats see people as fundamentally fragile and insist on more regulations to protect them
- New bureaucrats agree with more rules even if the rules are outside their specific field
  - HR managers responsible for diversity support new environmental regulations
  - Environmental activists advocate for more stringent compliance rules
  - Compliance experts argue in favor of more stringent mental health diagnoses and more spending on welfare
- The common thread is more rules, more intervention and more control
- Once the bureaucratic class reaches a critical mass, they can begin to exert market and social power in addition to regulatory influence
  - Refuse to shop at businesses that don't enthusiastically comply with bureaucratic norms
  - Socially ostracize people who have beliefs that are more oriented towards liberty or free markets
- The left's "march through the institutions" is itself an effect of the success these bureaucrats have had in changing the nature of the middle class

** The bureacratic class is destroying growth across the West
- In every G7 nation, growth has declined as this bureaucratic class has taken hold
- The average growth rate of the G7 has declined from 2.7% in 1980 to approximately 1% in the 2010s
- The partial exception to this decline has been the United States
  - US has benefited from vast supplies of cheap energy unlocked by fracking
  - However, this has been opposed by the US bureaucratic class
  - Even in the US, people with post-graduate degrees earning over $150,000 a year support
    - Rationing meat, gas and electricity to reduce the effects of climate change
    - Banning gas stoves
    - Banning gasoline automobiles
    - Banning non-essential air travel
    - Banning private air conditioning
    - /But, on the other hand, a far smaller proportion of people in the US have post-graduate degrees as compared with people in the UK/
    - /Here, thankfully, it's still possible to have a good life with "only" a bachelor's degree/
- Despite these narratives of austerity, the growth of the bureaucratic class has been accompanied by a significant increase in government debt and taxation
  - Taxes have increased by 3.5% as a share of GDP
  - Ratio of debt to GDP has doubled
- Legislation and regulation have spread at an accelerating rate
  - The number of federal regulations in the US has more than doubled since the 1970s
  - The total word count of Germany's laws is 60% higher than it was in the 1990s
  - The EU, between 2019 and 2024 passed 13,000 new regulations, double that of the US (which itself has seen a significant acceleration in the number of regulations)
  - In the UK, parliamentary acts of legislation rose from a few thousand in the postwar period to over 10,000 in the 2010s
- The bureaucratic class is incapable of reforming this ever growing thicket of legislation because its core assumption is that regulation is good and more regulation is better
  - Bureaucratic answers to problems always involve more central control
  - Always expanding the power and scope of the public sector
  - Giving more power to individuals is never considered
- This drives extremely weak public sector productivity growth --- public sector productivity has only improved by 0.2% per year over the past several decades

** The bureaucratic assault on capitalism
- The bureaucratic sector is not content with stopping public sector reform, they stop private sector reform too
- Planning new infrastructure is almost impossible
  - The planning application for the Lower Thames Crossing is 359,866 pages of paperwork
  - A 3.3 mile railway branch line required 79,187 pages of paperwork
  - The environmental impact assessment alone for the Sizewell C nuclear power station was 44,000 pages
- This is a failure of politics, not process
- Each specific regulation is seen as a positive, despite the overall weight of regulations making it impossible to build new transport or energy infrastructure
- We can understand the economic slowdown in the West using Acemoglu and Robinson's framework from /Why Nations Fail/
  - Inclusive, market-based institutions drive productivity and make goods and services cheaper
  - Extractive institutions block capitalism and redistribute wealth
  - The bureaucratic class are an extractive institution, forcing others to pay for them with legal sanctions and moral manipulation
- Businesses do not necessarily oppose the rise of the bureaucratic class because business dynamism has declined, especially in Europe
  - None of the top 100 European firms are less than 40 years old
  - Large companies dominate without growing or improving
  - The bureaucratic class and its ideology-driven regulations increase barriers to entry
  - People aspire to stable jobs in the bureaucratic class rather than productive entrepreneurship
- This is examined in detail by Lindsey and Teles in /The Captured Economy/
- Today's problem is different from the 1970s, where low profits were driven by socialist redistribution
- Instead, the problem is that profits are driven more by adherence to bureaucratic class whims rather than fulfilling market needs
- As the state grows, the quantity of regulations grows even as the quality of regulations deteriorates, enabling greater regulatory capture by existing market participants
- This is illustrated by the difference in prices and productivity between sectors where markets are stronger and sectors which are more subject to government regulation
  - For computers, a sector where market forces are dominant,  prices have fallen by 83% since 2000, allowing someone to buy the equivalent of a £1,000 PC in 2000 for just under £200 today
  - Over the same time period clothing prices dropped by 30%
  - In contrast housing prices have increased by 254%
  - Electricity prices have risen by 200%
  - College tuition has increased from £3,000 in 2000 to £27,000 today
- However, to solve these issues, the bureaucratic class never proposes fewer regulations
- The solution is always more government and more central control

** The progressive assault on our social fabric has economic costs
- The bureaucratic class damages the fabric of society as well as strangling economic growth
- From 1965 to 2015 there were massive advances in rights for LGBT people, liberalization around sexual norms, liberalization of abortion regulations, increases in women in the workplace, and declines in racism
- /Kind of funny that the Conservative party wants to take credit for all of these things, when many of them were passed over the stringent objections of Conservatives at the time/
- The right has accepted social change after social change in order to preserve social peace
- However, there is no amount of social change that is sufficient for the bureaucratic class
- The bureaucratic class creates endless new marginalized identities around race, gender, and the environment itself
- The progressive goal is to turn everything into a political question
- Wage constant war on supposedly neutral areas of life
  - Jokes
  - Knitting
  - Leisure activities, such as visiting the countryside
  - Ever-growing "safetyism" in university culture
- It is misleading to think of these as free-floating social issues
- The end-goal of the progressive project is to prime all individuals to see themselves as victims, and seek redress from the bureaucratic class
- Building an economy which encourages individual resiliency and risk-taking is incompatible with the progressive ideology that seeks to convince people that they are fragile victims

** The progressive assault on the democratic nation-state
- A key part of progressive politics is hostility to the democratic nation-state
  - Democratic institutions present an alternative pathway to power
  - The nation state is inherently discriminatory, by virtue of having fixed borders
  - These borders create a distinction between (privileged) citizens and (marginalized) outsiders
- This hostility to the nation-state is unprecedented in Western thought
- Prior thinkers, whether liberal or conservative have always taken the existence of nation-states as a given
- Strong national identities form the bedrock of economic and social development
- Weak national identities drive state dysfunction --- numerous African or Middle Eastern countries which appear homogenous from a distance are riven by internal discord because of weak national identities
- By contrast, states like Korea or Japan, which also underwent civil wars and great hardship managed to sustain development, thanks to their strong national identity
- /I would add to that that national identities can be created --- Germany and Italy both had national identities created for them by nationalists and revolutionaries/
- Leftists believe that assimilation and the attainment of legal and cultural entitlements are "investments in settler colonialism"
- /Which is funny because they're talking about the UK --- who exactly are the displaced natives? The druids?/
- The progressive goal is to replace the nation-state with a bureaucracy-state
- In practice, this will result in western democracies into post-colonial states with weak national identities
- Progressives are uncomfortable with judging people based on what benefits they can bring to the society that they're migrating to
- This forces states dominated by progressive ideologies to admit migrants in excess of what they can cope with

** Reacting whilst avoiding the reactionary trap
- Undoing the progressive bureaucratization of economics and politics does not imply rolling back all social changes and going back to how things were in an imagined ideal past
- Attemping to do so would be exceedingly difficult, politically
  - Only 6% of people disapprove of people remaining childless
  - Only 8% of people disapprove of cohabitation before marriage
  - 13% of people think homosexuality is morally wrong
  - 29% of people disapprove of cannabis
- Advocating for a wholesale return to the past would alienate vast numbers of people and would do nothing to roll back the ever creeping bureaucracy

** A new right is being born and Conservatives must renew or be replaced
- It cannot be taken as a given that middle-class voters will return to the Conservative party merely because a Labor government fails
- The Conservatives must
  - Focus on a strong national identity
  - Focus on streamlining bureaucracy
  - Take on supranational bodies encroaching on democratic control
    - /Okay, but you already did that when you left the EU/
- Have to do this without getting sidetracked into a reactionary ideology that will alienate potential middle-class supporters
