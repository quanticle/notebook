#+TITLE: Places vs. People: The Ins and Outs of Labor Market Adjustment to Globalization

* Introduction

- A major economic challenge is regionally-concentrated job losses
- Caused by
  - Globalization
  - Automation
  - Energy transition
- Locales that specialized in manufacturing for specific industries have lost their industrial core
- Leads to
  - Elevated mortality
  - Increased single-parenthood and poverty
  - Political extremism
- This paper examines the intertwined adjustments of labor markets and individual workers due to the shock of import competition from China
- China's disruption to labor markets came as a shock to policymakers and economists in the US
  - Initial impressions were that increased trade with low-wage countries would have only modest effects
  - Shock would be diffused across the entire economy due to labor market mobility
- However now we know that regions specialized in manufacturing felt intense pain from labor market shocks when faced with a large increase in import competition from China
- Decline in manufacturing employment was not offset by an increase in non-manufacturing employment
- Regional effects were observed in other high-income countries as well
  - Germany
  - Norway
  - Spain
  - France
  - United Kingdom
  - Italy
  - Australia
- Adverse labor market outcomes fell disproportionately on workers in industries that were exposed to competition from Chinese imports
- These workers had lower earnings growth
- However, net out-migration from areas exposed to trade was muted
- Data about local labor markets and individual workers is often misaligned
- Don't know how places adjusted to the China shock
  - Who are the new arrivals
  - How does the composition of industries and the quality of jobs change
  - How long do changes take
- Characterizing labor inflows and outflows can help us understand why places struggle in the aftermath of import shocks
- We also want to understand whether people adjust along with places
  - Do workers leave while regions languish
  - Do regions rebound while workers struggle
- Disentangling people and places can guide decisions about how aid should be delivered
  - Location based investment subsidies
  - Subsidies for worker training
- This study merges data from the Census Bureau's Longitudinal Employer Household Dynamics program with data from the American Community Survey and 2000 and 2010 decennial censuses
- Tracks changes in individuals' earnings and employment grouped by initial communting zone (CZ)
- Data tracks changes across two decades --- from 2000, 1 year before China joined the WTO to 2019, the year prior to the pandemic
- Trade-exposed commuting zones had persistent excess job losses in manufacturing
- However these losses were more than offset by gains in non-manufacturing by the end of the 20 year period
- These CZs had smaller net population outflows (or greater population inflows) --- lower employment to population ratio
- Non-manufacturing job gains were concentrated among
  - Women
  - College-educated
  - Minority racial and ethnic groups
  - Foreign-born
- Non-manufacturing jobs were concentrated in service industries with relatively low wage premia
  - Leisure and hospitality
  - Retail trade
  - Non-traded medical services
  - K-12 education
- Although trade-exposed locations recover their employment by the end of the data period, trade-exposed workers do not
  - Workers were more likely to take up lower-paid work outside of manufacturing
  - Less likely to leave and find work in another CZ
  - Maladjustment to import competition is particularly acute among workers who are
    - Non-college educated
    - Initially low-earning
    - Initially employed in low-wage premium industries
- Trade exposed places experience both a job recovery and increased joblessness
  - Employment growth is greater relative to the initial population
  - Employment prospects for incumbent workers are worse
- This is caused by the lack of out-migration in response to import competition
  - Trade-exposed places have smaller outflows of older workers
  - Larger inflows of new workers
  - New workers are disproportionately minorities and foreign-born
  - New generation of workers hold jobs that are
    - Relatively low-paid
    - Provide services to local families and non-working older residents

* Related Literature

- Prior episodes of trade shocks provided clues to the disruption caused by trade with China
- Workers that have been displaced by mass layoffs, regardless of their cause, tend to earn permanently less than otherwise similar workers
- Less-well educated workers were known to be less-likely to leave places following downturns
- The China shock combined these two patterns
  - Mass layoffs due to factory closures
  - Factories largely employed less-educated workers
- Manufacturing unemployment due to import competition was particularly severe in the US and UK because these countries experienced no corresponding increase in exports
- Losses were more limited in Germany and Switzerland, as they were able to increase exports to China
- Japan and South Korea actually experienced growth in manufacturing as they were able to leverage Chinese production intermediate goods to increase their production of final goods
- /So the US and UK just had uniquely bad policy responses to the China shock?/
- Recent literature embeds results from reduced-form empirical analysis into quantitative general equilibrium models in order to project changes in real income
  - Projections require the specification of adjustment mechanisms
  - The form of the adjustment is materially important for quantifying the distributional consequences of globalization
- Empirical results in this paper offer more support for models that allow for less labor mobility and more persistent unemployment
- The economic literature provides little evidence to support the use of import tariffs to combat regionalized joblessness
- However, place-based economic support policies may be effective if the right conditions are present
  - Spillover effects that cause workers to concentrate in larger, higher-wage regions
  - Market frictions that cause some workers to be stuck in smaller, lower-wage regions
- If the above conditions are present, localized adverse shocks may lead to a self-reinforcing process of deagglomeration and occupational downgrading among the newly unemployed
- This may dissuade new firms from entering locales affected by adverse shocks, widening spatial disparities in earnings and well-being
- A large literature has assessed the impact of the China shock over the medium term (i.e. 2000 to 2007)
- Much less attention has been paid to impacts beyond that point
- The evidence from medium-term assessments of the China shock indicates that
  - Local tax revenues are reduced, leading to reduced public services diminishing the attractiveness of places to businesses
  - Greater criminality, adverse health outcomes and mortality tied to risky behaviors, reducing labor productivity
  - Increases the fraction of children living in single-parent households, impeding the accumulation of human capital among the young

* Data Sources and Empirical Methods

** Data Sources

- Data is sourced from a set of administrative and demographic data sets from the Census Bureau
- Logitudinal Employer Household Dynamics (LEHD)
- Derived from state unemployment insurance (UI) records
- Employment History File (EHF) --- job-level database
- A job is a unique employer-employee combination
- Contains quarterly observations of earnings for almost all private wage and salaried workers in participating states
- This paper aggregates the EHF's quarterly data into annual data
- When workers change jobs during a year, the employer taken to be the employer that pays the most on an annualized basis
- Location data is based on locations of employers rather than worker residences
- The fact that data is aggregated by commuting zone (CZ) means that usually worker and employer locales are the same
- Sample period is 2000 to 2019
  - Starts just before China's accession to the WTO
  - Ends just before the COVID 19 pandemic
- Contains 43 states that consistently participated in the LEHD
- Restricted to workers with positive annual earnings between the ages of 18 and 64
- Earnings data is taken from the Opportunity Databank (OD), which aggregates tax data for earnings
- OD provides data on date of birth, sex, and foreign-born status
- Race and ethnic identity is derived from the American Community Survey
- Several analyses focus on the subset of workers for which ACS data has information on educational attainment levels
- Data makes use of import penetration by Chinese manufacturing by industry from 2000 to 2007 to track the impact of Chinese imports
- Uses Personal Consumption Expenditure Index from Bureau of Labor Statistics

** Changing Employment and Demographic Composition in Manufacturing and Non-Manufacturing

- The share of employment in the manufacturing sector in the US as a whole fell by more than \(\frac{1}{3}\) from 14.2% to 9.2% of employment
- Conversely the share of employment in health and education, leisure and hospitality, and business and professional services increased
- Other industries were largely unchanged
- A central contributor to the decline of manufacturing employment was competition from China
- This longitudinal analysis investigates
  - Whether individuals who lost jobs in manufacturing found jobs in other sectors
  - Whether CZs that lost manufacturing employment gained employment in other sectors
  - How did this structural transformation affect different demographics
  - Were non-manufacturing jobs comparable to lost manufacturing jobs in terms of earnings
- Manufacturing and non-manufacturing employment have very different demographics
- Manufacturing workers tend to be
  - Older
  - Male
  - Slightly more likely to be white
  - Slightly less likely to be black
  - Slightly more likely to be hispanic
- Historically manufacturing employment has paid relatively high wages to non-college educated workers
- Manufacturing remained a high-wage sector, even in 2019
  - In 2000 46% of manufacturing workers were in the top tercile of earnings, as compared with 31% of non-manufacturing workers
  - By 2019 54% of manufacturing workers were in the top tercile of earnings, as compared with 38% of non-manufacturing workers
  - /So even as the total amount of manufacturing employment declined, the relative pay of manufacturing workers increased/
  - /In fact the pay divergence between manufacturing and non-manufacturing increased from 2000 to 2019, even as manufacturing employment declined/
